# SELL-BY DATE — Dialogue Delivery Blueprint

The spec every line is checked against. Built from real reference dialogue (sources noted) so the writing stops being a guessing game.

---

## 0. The five laws (every line must pass all five)
1. **Picturable.** Names a concrete thing you can see, or a specific number/object. Ban abstractions ("we disappear," "that's a market," "it works").
2. **Literal, never a riddle.** No pun-as-logic, no line you must decode ("that hand was wearing 1684").
3. **Causes the next line** (*but / therefore*), never a list of quips.
4. **Funny from character or situation, not cleverness.** If the joke is the *word*, cut it. If it's the *person's priority*, keep it.
5. **Premise never assumed; exposition only via the "English, Doc!" device** (§2). No info-dumps.

---

## 1. Character voice cards

### DREW — model: Don Draper (Mad Men)
- **Comic engine:** misplaced commercial priority — meets catastrophe, sees pipeline.
- **Steers toward:** the sale, the money, the close.
- **Shape:** short, confident; reframes any objection into an opportunity; impatient with detail.
- **Reference line:** *"What you call love was invented by guys like me, to sell nylons."*
- **DO:** "He paid retail." / "Dead men don't cancel." — **DON'T:** abstract marketing-speak ("no ad blockers").

### KAI — model: Russ Hanneman (Silicon Valley)
- **Comic engine:** reckless-builder pride — treats a disaster as a successful product launch.
- **Steers toward:** scale, shipping, "it works."
- **Reference line:** *"Amazon lost money every quarter for twenty years."*
- **DO:** "And it works." / "What's our burn rate on gold?" — **DON'T:** fear or caution (Margo's lane).

### MARGO — model: Ian Malcolm (Jurassic Park) + Doc Brown (Back to the Future)
- **Comic engine:** the accurate genius nobody listens to. Real jargon, then forced into plain English (§2).
- **Steers toward:** consequences, the rules.
- **Reference line:** *"Life finds a way."* (smart = vivid + plain, not robotic).
- **DO:** real dynamical-systems jargon → blunt **specific** kicker. — **DON'T:** vague doom ("we disappear").

### PERIOD GUESTS (kings, generals, courtiers) — model: Blackadder / our Louis
- **Comic engine:** grand, vain, enormous overreaction; elaborate wit.
- **Reference line:** *"…we buried what was left of him in a soup tureen."*
- **DO:** "I can hear colours." / "We invade someone. Today." — **DON'T:** modern phrasing.

## 2. The exposition device — "English, Doc!"
How every rule gets explained without an info-dump (from *Back to the Future*):

> Doc: "…a new temporal event sequence resulting in this alternate reality." / Marty: **"English, Doc!"** / Doc: [plain]

**Mandatory pattern:**
1. Margo delivers it in **real jargon** (proves she's smart).
2. Drew/Kai cut her off: **"English."** (the laugh + the reset).
3. Margo lands the plain version — **concrete and specific, never vague** (§3).

---

## 3. The specificity rule (fixes "we disappear")
A plain consequence names a **picturable, specific** outcome:
- ❌ "We change too much, we disappear." (vague)
- ✅ "Sell too much, and we were never born." (specific, picturable)
- ✅ "Three more sales like that, there's no agency. No Tuesday. No *us*."

---

## 4. Comic mechanics library (named moves)
- **Comply-first-doubt-second** — *"Then so do I. …Because?"* (Better Off Ted)
- **The dark topper** — *"It tastes like despair." / "Is it possible it just needs salt?"* (Better Off Ted)
- **Euphemism for the monstrous** — *"his being dead may have been a pre-existing condition."* (Better Off Ted)
- **The Draper reframe** — object → opportunity/feeling.
- **The cut-off** — kill a line mid-sentence ("Bring me a—" + blade).
- **The grand overreaction** — period guest treats a small thing as world-ending.

---

## 5. Line-construction checklist (pass/fail before any line ships)
- Picturable? (concrete noun/image)
- Literal? (no decoding)
- In-voice? (matches the card)
- Causes the next beat?
- Funny from character/situation, not a quip?
- Specific consequence named (no vague abstraction)?
