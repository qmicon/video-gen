# TV Development Reference — Concept, Story & Pitch

A working reference for the ViralAdsGen video-gen pipeline, distilled from StudioBinder's **"How to Develop a TV Show"** series (the transcriptions in this folder). Where the `shotlist transcriptions` README covers *how to shoot* (the visual/technical craft), this one covers *how to build the story and sell it* — concept, character, structure, the one-line hook, and the pitch.

**Why it matters for ad work:** a high-performing video ad is a tiny TV pilot. It has to open on a hook, introduce a relatable protagonist, run a problem→solution arc, and end on a question/CTA — then prove the concept could sustain a whole campaign. Every framework below maps onto that. See the **[Applied to ad creative](#applied-to-ad-creative)** section for the mapping.

**Grounding rule:** every item is extracted directly from the transcriptions. Each is tagged with its source episode (e.g. `[Ep3]` = `04_Ep3_Pilot_Script.txt`). Quoted phrases are verbatim. Nothing is invented; the *ad-creative* application notes are clearly marked as the framing layer.

> **Core principle (from the whole series):** an *idea* is not a *story*. "The King died and then the Queen died is a story. The King died and then the Queen died of grief is plot." `[Ep1]` The work of development is turning a kernel into something with **legs** — "entertaining and interesting beyond the pilot." `[Ep1]`

# PART 1 — TREATMENT: is my concept a story? `[Ep1]`

The treatment is where you "answer a simple question: is my concept a story?" Test for **legs** — whether it's "entertaining and interesting beyond the pilot." "Not every great idea is a great story with an active engaging plot."

**Start at the end.** "The best pilots tend to start at the end." Frame the idea so it "ends a normal state of life, to begin a new course, and then ends again with the compelling question." The rhythm: **Ending → Beginning → Ending.** "We end an old life to begin a new one" and "end the pilot with a question that establishes the rest of the show."

**Lay the groundwork.** "Laying the groundwork for [the] entire series… is something that every pilot tries to do, but very few actually do." Develop the world and the characters to "very clearly indicate… this is who this person is and this is what they want." Give each character "a global drive… to go over the series" so the audience senses "where the show's going to go."

**Worked examples:**
- *Cheers* ("a perfect pilot") — "everything that happens happens for a reason"; the way you're introduced to the people previews the whole series. Ends on the "will they / won't they" question.
- *Justified* — Raylan must "end his nice sunny life in Miami" and begin again in his hometown, ending the pilot on: "Can Raylan ever escape his violent nature?"
- *Friday Night Lights* — a "normal day in the life" (first day of football season) introduces the ensemble through media questions; the injury that ends Jason Street's story leaves the cliffhanger: "Who will Jason Street be from now on?"

**Rule of thumb:** "the more groundwork you lay before you start scripting, the stronger your pilot will be."

# PART 2 — CHARACTER & ARC: Who / What / Where / Want `[Ep2]`

Characters are "the reason audiences keep coming back." "The best characters [are] people that have to constantly deal with things and that's how we see who they really are." Build them with four questions (worked example: *Mad Men* / Don Draper):

1. **Who** — "Who is the story about?" The defining surface (Don: ad exec, heavy drinker, womanizer… and secretly Dick Whitman). Find the entryway into how they think.
2. **What** — "What has happened… what is happening… what will happen" to the character. Layer backstory (he "stole a dead man's identity") with a present hook (the pilot reveals he's a family man cheating on his wife — "right from there you're hooked").
3. **Where** — "Most shows build their ensembles around location." "You let the location dictate some of the people that might be in your world, and then you can build who they are." (Cheers = people in a bar; Mad Men = a 1960s NY ad agency → the macho men, the secretaries, a Peggy with gumption, a Joan who secretly runs the office.)
4. **Want** — the relationship layer: "How [do] these characters relate to create dynamic?" Define what each character *wants from another* (What does Roger want from Don? What does Betty want from Don?). "You can go on and on between all the characters."

**Method note:** when layering a character, "think a lot about what would I do in that situation?" — sometimes "picking the not-so-great parts of yourself… what's the most desperate version of me doing?"

# PART 3 — PILOT STRUCTURE: the but/therefore engine `[Ep3]`

**The South Park rule** (Trey Parker & Matt Stone): scenes should not connect with "and then… and then." "It should be: this happens, **therefore** this happens, **but** this happens, therefore this happens." Causality, not sequence. (Worked example: the *Breaking Bad* pilot.)

**Three-act build:**
- **Act 1 — establish, then the inciting incident.** First establish the normal world (Walt is a chemistry teacher "walked all over," bullied, "pushed around in his own family"). The **inciting incident** is "what kicks off the series… the event that changes his life forever" (the cancer diagnosis) — "typically that first ending."
- **Act 2 — active drive to a climax.** "Our hero needs an active drive that builds to a climax." Don't skip steps (Walt needs an accomplice → blackmails Jesse). "Act two must culminate in a climax — think of the worst thing that could happen to your protagonist." "Good writing is showcased by how well you can get your protagonist out of it."
- **Act 3 — the denouement.** "A fancy French word that literally means the act of untying." The Act 2 climax "propels us into act 3." "Oftentimes a pilot's denouement will end with a **cliffhanger** that sets up the rest of the series" and suggests the change the series will explore.

# PART 4 — LOGLINE: two formulas `[Ep4]`

"The logline is a sentence that encapsulates what your pilot is about." Two reliable formulas:

**Formula A — Inciting Incident + Protagonist + Action + Antagonist.**
- *Inciting incident* — what ends the protagonist's normality and "sets off the main plot."
- *Protagonist* — the main character; in an ensemble, "the catalyst character that everyone revolves around" (JD in *Scrubs*, Michael in *Arrested Development*).
- *Action* — "what [the] protagonist has to do."
- *Antagonist* — "what's standing in the way of their action."
- (*Stranger Things*: Will's disappearance [incident] → the town/friends rally [protagonist + action] → they must fight "this guy" [antagonist].)

**Formula B — Protagonist + Action + Antagonist + Goal + Stake.** Drops the inciting incident, adds *goal* ("the reason for the protagonist['s] action," making the action more specific) and *stake*. (*Star Wars*: an orphan kid joins the Rebels to fight Darth Vader and rescue a princess "from the hands of certain death.")

**Specificity wins.** "Studio executives read hundreds of loglines." Add "personality, color, texture and tone." "An orphan boy" → "a spirited farm boy." A generic shark logline → "a local sheriff, a marine biologist, and a salty seafarer form an unlikely team that must stop a Great White Shark from taking more lives and ruining an entire beach community." "Using specificity allows for personality and tone… the small details that we relate to more personally."

# PART 5 — THE SHOW BIBLE: seven components `[Ep5]`

The bible helps you "develop and sell your series." Goal in the room: get executives "to ask a lot of questions" — "You don't want to be telling them the show. You want them to say 'Is this like this and that?' and you want to say, 'Yes, and.'"

Seven components:
1. **One-page pitch** — "your first impression"; "lead with your logline."
2. **Logline** — "broken down into one sentence… the first line['s] got to tell them everything. The logline can never be rewritten enough."
3. **Tone** — convey style and genre with familiar visuals; be able to answer "What is your show like?" by crossing concepts (e.g. "a political drama mixed with a horror-comedy… West Wing with Walking Dead"). "Keep them guessing."
4. **Character breakdowns** — "about three to four character descriptions… simplicity is the most important thing." Define key relationships, pick a star for the lead, give "less space to lesser characters," and define each character's "wants and… needs." Add "a statement, like a quote from the character… their catchphrase" ("These violent delights have violent ends").
5. **Future episodes / seasons** — "executives want [the] show to be sustainable… create the feeling that the show could go on forever." "5 to 6 [episode ideas] is perfect," "boiled down to the bare essentials."
6. **World-building** — "the environment that you're in… how your character is going to evolve." "Almost inhabit that world… sit in that world and spend some time."
7. **Mood board** — "collect all of these inspiring elements and present them together."

# PART 6 — THE PITCH: delivery + the 7-point map `[Ep6]`

**Delivery — three things to keep in mind:**
1. **Be brief** — "get to the point."
2. **Find an emotional hook** — "the tone of your pitch should match the tone of the show."
3. **Be confident** — "you have to have passion for your idea… that's contagious."

**The pitch map (7 beats):**
1. **The Origin** — "what inspired you to write this idea."
2. **The Human Hook** — "Why will the audience care?"
3. **The Characters** — "Who is this about? Where are they in their lives?"
4. **Desires** — "What do they want right now?"
5. **Obstacles** — "What's in the way?"
6. **The Highlight Reel** — "the funny, sad, bonkers moments that should stand out and be memorable."
7. **The Open Road** — "What can we expect to see from the rest of the series?"

(The episode demonstrates the full map as a spoken *Breaking Bad* pitch — opening on direct audience questions: "Have you ever had trouble providing for your family? Do you wonder if they'd be taken care of when you die?")

> "Luck is when opportunity meets preparation." `[Ep6]`

# PART 7 — THE SIZZLE REEL: sell by showing `[Ep7]`

When the pitch/bible/script alone don't land, "a sizzle that shows off both your concept and your ability to make it work" lets you "double down on your hand." "Any time they can look at something, as opposed to reading it, you're better off."

- **Show the opening, not everything.** "It's very important to just be talking about the pilot… make it like this is the opening to your show… not give them too much but… the best idea you can visually."
- **Use what you have.** *It's Always Sunny* was shot on "VHS tapes and an old camcorder," selling on "acting and comedic chops, instead of set pieces." *Another Period* was pitched as "Keeping Up With The Kardashians meets Turn of the Century" via a short — execs "saw their ability to act, write, direct and produce."
- **Build an online body of work.** "It's always smart to put stuff on YouTube… have an online presence." Web series → network deals: *High Maintenance* (Vimeo → HBO), *Insecure* (Issa Rae's "Awkward Black Girl" sketches → HBO). "Is your idea something that people are going to click on or want to see."
- **Crowdfunding** as a proof signal: "all of these properties were crowdfunded, and all of them went on to see mainstream success."
- **Mindset:** "For the first pitch, you're probably going to fail… the best thing about pitching is to fail and then afterwards figure out what you did wrong. There's always gonna be another opportunity, as long as you stay in the game." Showing initiative "can be the thing that turns that no into a yes."

# APPLIED TO AD CREATIVE
*The framing layer — how each TV-development tool maps onto a video ad. (Application is our synthesis; the underlying definitions are sourced above.)*

| TV-dev tool | Source | Use in a video ad |
|---|---|---|
| **Legs test** ("is my concept a story?") | `[Ep1]` | Does the angle sustain a *campaign* of variations, or is it a one-off? Kill concepts without legs early. |
| **Start at the end / Ending→Beginning→Ending** | `[Ep1]` | Open on the after-state or the rupture (the hook), not the slow setup. End on an implied question/CTA. |
| **Who / What / Where / Want** | `[Ep2]` | Build the ad's protagonist = the customer avatar. *What* = their pain (before) and transformation (after); *Where* = the relatable setting; *Want* = the desire the offer fulfills. |
| **But / Therefore (not "and then")** | `[Ep3]` | Each beat must *cause* the next: problem → *therefore* attempt → *but* obstacle → *therefore* solution. Kills boring, list-like ads. |
| **Inciting incident → active drive → climax → cliffhanger** | `[Ep3]` | Hook (the rupture) → demonstrate the solution → payoff (the result) → CTA as the "what happens next." |
| **Logline (specificity)** | `[Ep4]` | The ad's one-line value prop / on-screen hook. Swap generic for specific: "a farm boy," not "a boy"; concrete customer + concrete outcome. |
| **Tone = "X meets Y"** | `[Ep5]` | Define the ad's reference tone in one line for the brief ("luxury spa meets science lab"). Keeps generation on-brand. |
| **Catchphrase / character quote** | `[Ep5]` | The memorable spoken line or tagline the viewer repeats. |
| **Sustainability / future episodes** | `[Ep5]` | A winning ad concept should spawn a content series, not a single asset. |
| **Pitch map (Origin → Human Hook → Characters → Desires → Obstacles → Highlight Reel → Open Road)** | `[Ep6]` | A ready-made 7-beat script skeleton for a VSL or long-form ad. The *Human Hook* = "why will the audience care" in the first 3 seconds. |
| **Be brief / emotional hook / confident** | `[Ep6]` | Ad delivery: lead with the hook, match tone to emotion, sell with conviction. |
| **Sizzle reel: show the opening, use what you have, build online proof** | `[Ep7]` | The ad *is* the sizzle. Lead with the strongest visual, test scrappy UGC-style cuts, and treat every posted variation as a proof asset. "Is your idea something people are going to click on?" |

**Stacked workflow for an ad concept:** test for *legs* `[Ep1]` → define the protagonist with *Who/What/Where/Want* `[Ep2]` → structure beats with *but/therefore* and the *inciting-incident arc* `[Ep3]` → compress to a specific *logline* hook `[Ep4]` → set *tone* in one "X meets Y" line `[Ep5]` → lay the script on the *7-beat pitch map* `[Ep6]` → ship it as a *sizzle*, leading with the strongest visual `[Ep7]`.
